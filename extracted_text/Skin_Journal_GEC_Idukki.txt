# WaveCoAtNet: Wavelet-Guided Frequency-Decomposed Cross-Attention and Prototype-Anchored Token Selection for Parameter-Efficient Classification of Ichthyosis Variants in Dermatological Images

---

## Abstract

The ichthyoses comprise a group of rare, hereditary genodermatoses that present profound diagnostic challenges owing to extreme inter-class morphological overlap, severe class imbalance, and the data scarcity intrinsic to rare-disease imaging. Accurate, early computational phenotyping is critical for time-sensitive intervention, yet standard networks struggle to disentangle the fine textural cues (e.g., the polygonal scaling of Ichthyosis Vulgaris) from the macro-structural cues (e.g., the plate-like fissures of Harlequin Ichthyosis) that separate these conditions. We introduce **WaveCoAtNet**, a hybrid convolutional–transformer architecture engineered for morphological disambiguation in low-data regimes. WaveCoAtNet couples a preserved ConvNeXt-Tiny convolutional path with a parallel transformer path driven by five mechanisms: (i) **Wavelet-Guided Frequency-Decomposed Cross-Attention (WG-FDCA)**, which uses a 2-D Haar transform to separate structural (low-frequency) from textural (high-frequency) evidence and fuses them through a gated cross-attention; (ii) **Prototype-Anchored Dynamic Token Selection (PA-DTS)**, which scores tokens against EMA-tracked class prototypes; (iii) **Prototype-Guided Attention Pooling (PGAP)**; (iv) **Dual-Path Aggregation (DPA)**; and (v) **Supervised Contrastive Token Regularization (SCTR)**. We evaluate on a curated five-class dataset of **2,508** dermatological images. Under stratified 5-fold cross-validation, WaveCoAtNet achieves a mean accuracy of **96.25% ± 0.81%** (95% CI [95.54, 96.97]), macro-F1 of **0.9550 ± 0.0090**, and Cohen's κ of **0.9514**. On a controlled fixed-split benchmark of twelve pretrained and from-scratch models trained under an identical protocol, WaveCoAtNet is the **top-performing model** (91.14% accuracy, macro-F1 0.8697), ahead of CoAtNet (90.51%), Swin-T (89.87%) and the foundation models DINOv2 (89.24%) and BiomedCLIP (87.97%), while using **2.7×–6.2× fewer parameters** than the large transformer baselines. We report that the margins among the top models are within the resolution of a 158-image test set (McNemar p > 0.3), which is precisely why we adopt cross-validation as the primary protocol. A ten-condition ablation isolates WG-FDCA as the single most impactful component (macro-F1 −3.34% when replaced by plain cross-attention). WaveCoAtNet thus offers a favorable accuracy–efficiency trade-off and a reproducible benchmark for rare skin-disease classification.

**Keywords:** Ichthyosis; Rare disease; Wavelet transform; Hybrid CNN–Transformer; Cross-attention; Prototype learning; Supervised contrastive learning; Medical image classification

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 Clinical background and motivation

The ichthyoses constitute a highly heterogeneous group of Mendelian disorders of cornification, characterized by widespread, persistent hyperkeratosis, scaling, and profound epidermal-barrier dysfunction. Pathogenetically, these genodermatoses arise from distinct mutations disrupting the epidermal differentiation complex—*TGM1* in Lamellar Ichthyosis (LI), *FLG* in Ichthyosis Vulgaris (IV), *SPINK5* in Netherton Syndrome (NS), and *ABCA12* in Harlequin Ichthyosis (HI) [1]. Despite these distinct molecular etiologies, the resulting cutaneous phenotypes exhibit extreme morphological overlap. The clinical spectrum ranges from mild, highly prevalent presentations (IV, ≈1 in 250) to ultra-rare, life-threatening neonatal emergencies requiring immediate intensive care (HI, ≈1 in 300,000). This drastic variance in incidence, coupled with the visual similarity among genetically distinct entities, establishes ichthyosis classification as a tremendously challenging domain for both clinicians and computational systems.

Current diagnostic workflows rely heavily on subjective clinical evaluation, occasionally supplemented by histopathology or protracted genomic sequencing. These paradigms are constrained by inter-observer variability, limited global access to specialized dermatogenetic expertise, and the time-critical nature of neonatal disease management. Consequently, patients frequently endure a protracted "diagnostic odyssey." There is an urgent, unmet need for rapid, automated computational phenotyping capable of delivering accurate, objective morphological stratification to accelerate targeted intervention and genetic counseling [2, 7].

### 1.2 The computational challenge

While deep learning—particularly Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) and Vision Transformers (ViTs)—has transformed the diagnosis of common dermatological malignancies such as melanoma [5, 6, 9], its translation to rare genodermatoses remains underdeveloped [7, 10]. The application of standard architectures to ichthyosis classification is impeded by two fundamental bottlenecks:

1. **Severe, naturally-occurring class imbalance** intrinsic to rare-disease epidemiology, where the rarest subtypes contribute an order of magnitude fewer images than the common ones.
2. **Fine-grained morphological overlap**, where the discriminative signal lies in the *frequency content* of the lesion—low-frequency structure (deep, plate-like fissures) versus high-frequency texture (fine, adherent scaling). Conventional networks treat these critical micro-textures as high-frequency noise, leading to confusion of rare variants, especially the morphologically adjacent LI–NS pair.

Furthermore, the profound data scarcity of rare diseases penalizes pure ViTs, which lack local inductive biases and require large-scale corpora to converge [22]. These observations motivate an architecture that (a) retains convolutional inductive bias for data efficiency, (b) performs *explicit* frequency decomposition so neither structure nor texture is suppressed, and (c) actively enforces inter-class separability under imbalance.

### 1.3 Contributions

This work makes four contributions to computational dermatology and rare-disease diagnostics:

1. **A curated rare-disease benchmark.** We compile, standardize, and document a dataset of **2,508** high-resolution images spanning five categories (four ichthyosis variants and healthy skin), providing a reproducible benchmark for computational phenotyping.
2. **The WaveCoAtNet architecture.** We propose a hybrid network whose parallel topology preserves a pretrained convolutional path while enriching it with a transformer path built from five custom mechanisms—WG-FDCA, PA-DTS, PGAP, DPA, and SCTR—together with CBAM feature recalibration, adding only ≈3.9M parameters of novel modules over the 27.8M ConvNeXt-Tiny backbone.
3. **Rigorous, honestly-reported evaluation.** We benchmark twelve models under an identical fixed-split protocol, report stratified 5-fold cross-validation with confidence intervals for the proposed model, conduct a ten-condition ablation, and apply McNemar significance testing throughout—reporting where differences are *not* significant.
4. **Architectural interpretability.** We isolate the contribution of each component and show that explicit frequency decomposition (WG-FDCA) is the principal driver of performance, while characterizing the limits of single-split evaluation on small rare-disease cohorts.

We are explicit about scope: WaveCoAtNet is the **best-performing** model on our fixed-split benchmark and exhibits **stable, high cross-validated accuracy**; because baselines were evaluated on the fixed split and the cross-validation protocol was applied to the proposed model, the two headline numbers (91.14% and 96.25%) are not to be read as a single head-to-head margin (see §4.2, §5.4, §7).

## 2. Related Work

### 2.1 Deep learning in dermatology

Computational dermatology is dominated by work on common conditions—melanoma and inflammatory lesions—using CNNs and, increasingly, transformers [5, 6, 9, 25]. Systematic reviews consistently highlight a deficit in methods for rare genetic skin disorders [7, 10]. Existing ichthyosis-specific work is limited to small-scale classical methods—for example, SVM classification of Ichthyosis Vulgaris [8]—or to clinical severity scoring rather than multi-class subtype discrimination [11, 12]. We provide a systematic multi-class deep-learning benchmark across five categories.

### 2.2 Hybrid convolutional–transformer architectures

Hybrid models combine the local inductive bias of convolution with the global receptive field of self-attention. CoAtNet [21] and UniFormer [16] unify depthwise convolution and attention; ConvNeXt [17] modernizes the pure-convolutional design with patchified stems, large kernels, and inverted-bottleneck blocks [18]. WaveCoAtNet adopts a ConvNeXt-Tiny backbone but departs from these models by decomposing intermediate features in the **frequency domain** before cross-attention, and by preserving the pretrained convolutional path in a parallel branch to retain transferable features under data scarcity.

### 2.3 Frequency-domain representation learning

Wavelet and Fourier representations have long supported denoising, super-resolution, and texture analysis. The 2-D discrete wavelet transform decomposes a feature map into one low-frequency (approximation) and three high-frequency (detail) sub-bands, providing a natural separation of structure from texture. WaveCoAtNet integrates a lightweight Haar transform inside a cross-attention block so that the network attends to structural and textural streams independently—aligning the computation with the diagnostic cue dermatologists use to distinguish fissures from scales.

### 2.4 Token selection and pruning

Token-reduction methods such as gradient-based pruning and learned token sparsification [27, 69] discard low-saliency tokens to reduce computation. The Gradient Focal Transformer (GFT) applies a progressive pruning schedule retaining 75%→50%→25% of tokens [50]. PA-DTS differs fundamentally: it scores tokens against *learnable class prototypes* updated by EMA, coupling selection to class semantics rather than to generic, class-agnostic saliency, and it predicts an image-specific retention ratio rather than using a fixed schedule.

### 2.5 Metric, prototype, and contrastive learning under imbalance

Supervised contrastive learning [20] and prototype-based representations improve class separability when data are scarce and imbalanced. SCTR applies a supervised contrastive objective to pooled token embeddings as an auxiliary regularizer that complements class-weighted, label-smoothed cross-entropy, while a cross-prototype orthogonality penalty prevents prototype collapse. This combination is designed to widen the margin between the historically confusable LI and NS classes.

### 2.6 Foundation models for medical imaging

Self-supervised and vision–language foundation models—DINOv2 [24] and BiomedCLIP [25]—offer powerful transferable features. However, recent evidence indicates that specialized foundation models do not always beat well-tuned supervised baselines on narrow tasks [67]. We include both as strong baselines and show that a compact, task-specific hybrid can match or exceed them at a fraction of the parameter cost.

## 3. Methodology

### 3.1 Overall architecture and parallel-fusion strategy

**[Figure 1 Placeholder: WaveCoAtNet architecture diagram — file `wavecoatnet_architecture.png` / `.pdf`]**

WaveCoAtNet uses a parallel-path design that preserves pretrained CNN features while enriching them with a novel transformer path. Given an input image $x \in \mathbb{R}^{3\times 224 \times 224}$, a ConvNeXt-Tiny stem and stages produce hierarchical features with channel progression $96 \to 192 \to 384 \to 768$. After the shared stem and the first two stages, the representation branches:

- **Global ViT pathway.** Stage-1 and Stage-2 features are fused via WG-FDCA (§3.2), augmented with a learnable positional embedding, and processed by standard Transformer self-attention blocks to model long-range dependencies.
- **Local CNN pathway.** Stage-2 features continue through Stages 3 and 4 of the pretrained backbone. Because deep stages accumulate spatial noise, Convolutional Block Attention Modules (CBAM, §3.7) recalibrate the feature maps after Stages 3 and 4.

The two pathways are unified via residual enrichment. ViT tokens $X_{\text{ViT}}$ are projected to the deep CNN channel dimension ($C=768$), spatially pooled to the $7\times7$ CNN grid, and added to the CNN tokens $X_{\text{CNN}}$:

$$X_{\text{fused}} = X_{\text{CNN}} + \mathrm{Pool}\big(\mathrm{Proj}_{\text{ViT}}(X_{\text{ViT}})\big).$$

The network then isolates diagnostically salient regions via PA-DTS (§3.3) and PGAP (§3.4), preserves holistic context via DPA (§3.5), and—during training—applies SCTR (§3.6) and a prototype-orthogonality penalty. The complete model contains **31,690,772** trainable parameters, of which ≈3.9M belong to the novel modules over the 27.8M ConvNeXt-Tiny backbone. (Compact and 3D renderings of the architecture are provided in Supplementary Figs. S1–S2.)

### 3.2 Wavelet-Guided Frequency-Decomposed Cross-Attention (WG-FDCA)

Let $X_{\text{stage1}} \in \mathbb{R}^{C \times H \times W}$ with $C=96$. A single-level 2-D Haar Discrete Wavelet Transform decomposes it into four sub-bands,

$$ \mathrm{LL},\ \mathrm{LH},\ \mathrm{HL},\ \mathrm{HH} = \mathrm{DWT}_{2D}(X_{\text{stage1}}), \qquad \text{each} \in \mathbb{R}^{C \times \frac{H}{2} \times \frac{W}{2}}, $$

where the approximation band $\mathrm{LL}$ forms the **low-frequency structural stream** (macro-patterns such as the plate-like fissures of HI) and the detail bands are concatenated along the channel axis, $[\mathrm{LH};\mathrm{HL};\mathrm{HH}] \in \mathbb{R}^{3C \times \frac{H}{2}\times\frac{W}{2}}$, to form the **high-frequency textural stream** (fine fish-scale patterns of IV). Each stream is projected by a batch-normalized $1\times1$ convolution to the Stage-2 dimension $C'=192$:

$$ K_{\text{low}} = \phi_{\text{low}}(\mathrm{LL}), \qquad K_{\text{high}} = \phi_{\text{high}}([\mathrm{LH};\mathrm{HL};\mathrm{HH}]). $$

Stage-2 features supply the queries $Q$. Two parallel multi-head cross-attention operations attend to each stream:

$$ \mathrm{Attn}_{\ast} = \mathrm{softmax}\!\left(\frac{Q K_{\ast}^{\top}}{\sqrt{d_h}}\right) V_{\ast}, \qquad \ast \in \{\text{low}, \text{high}\}, $$

with $d_h$ the per-head dimension. A learnable per-token **frequency gate**, an MLP with sigmoid activation $\sigma$, produces a content-adaptive scalar $\alpha_i \in (0,1)$ from the concatenated attention outputs:

$$ \alpha_i = \sigma\big(\mathrm{MLP}([\mathrm{Attn}_{\text{low}} \,\Vert\, \mathrm{Attn}_{\text{high}}])\big), $$

and the streams are fused, residually added to the queries, and refined by a feed-forward network (FFN):

$$ \mathrm{Attn}_{\text{fused}} = (1-\alpha)\odot \mathrm{Attn}_{\text{low}} + \alpha \odot \mathrm{Attn}_{\text{high}}, \qquad Z = Q + \mathrm{Attn}_{\text{fused}} + \mathrm{FFN}\big(\mathrm{LN}(Q + \mathrm{Attn}_{\text{fused}})\big). $$

This explicitly allows the network to shift focus between macro-structure and micro-texture according to the variant being analyzed, rather than mixing them in a single attention map.

### 3.3 Prototype-Anchored Dynamic Token Selection (PA-DTS)

PA-DTS anchors token selection to $C_{\text{cls}}=5$ learnable class prototypes $P_c \in \mathbb{R}^{D}$, refined during training by an Exponential Moving Average (EMA) and constrained by an orthogonality loss. Given token embeddings $x_i$, we compute $u_i = \mathrm{LayerNorm}(x_i)$, $\hat{x}_i = u_i/\lVert u_i\rVert_2$, and normalized prototypes $\hat{P}_c$. Three signals form the importance score:

**1. Prototype affinity** — maximal cosine similarity to a prototype:
$$ S_{\text{aff},i} = \max_{c} (\hat{x}_i \cdot \hat{P}_c). $$

**2. Affinity entropy** — Shannon entropy of the temperature-scaled similarity distribution ($T$ learnable), quantifying discriminative certainty:
$$ p_{i,c} = \frac{\exp\big((\hat{x}_i\cdot\hat{P}_c)/T\big)}{\sum_{j}\exp\big((\hat{x}_i\cdot\hat{P}_j)/T\big)}, \qquad S_{\text{ent},i} = -\sum_{c} p_{i,c}\log p_{i,c}. $$

**3. Channel attention** — an SE-style score on LayerNormed tokens, $S_{\text{ch},i} = \mathrm{MLP}_{\text{SE}}(u_i)$.

The signals are z-normalized ($\mathcal{Z}$) and fused with learnable softmax weights $\tilde{w}=\mathrm{softmax}(w)$:
$$ I_i = \mathrm{softmax}_i\Big( \tilde{w}_1 \mathcal{Z}(S_{\text{aff}})_i + \tilde{w}_2 \mathcal{Z}(S_{\text{ent}})_i + \tilde{w}_3 \mathcal{Z}(S_{\text{ch}})_i \Big). $$

The retention ratio is **predicted per image** from the globally pooled feature $\bar{x}$ and importance statistics:
$$ \texttt{keep} = k_{\min} + \sigma\big(\mathrm{MLP}([\bar{x}\,\Vert\,\mu(I)\,\Vert\,\sigma(I)\,\Vert\,\max(I)])\big)\cdot(k_{\max}-k_{\min}), \quad k_{\min}=0.6,\ k_{\max}=0.95. $$

The top-$K$ tokens are retained and residually amplified by their importance, $\tilde{x}_i = x_i\,(1+I_i)$. Prototypes are updated as $P_c \leftarrow m P_c + (1-m)\bar{e}_c$, with warm-up momentum $m=0.9$ for the first five epochs and $m=0.99$ thereafter; a penalty $\mathcal{L}_{\text{ortho}} = \lVert \hat{P}\hat{P}^{\top} - I\rVert^2$ discourages collapse.

### 3.4 Prototype-Guided Attention Pooling (PGAP)

PGAP supersedes unweighted mean-pooling by aggregating retained tokens in proportion to diagnostic relevance. For each retained token a relevance scalar is recomputed against detached prototypes,
$$ r_i = \max_{c}\left(\frac{\mathrm{LayerNorm}(\tilde{x}_i)}{\lVert\mathrm{LayerNorm}(\tilde{x}_i)\rVert_2}\cdot \hat{P}_c\right), \qquad \beta_i = \frac{\exp(r_i)}{\sum_j \exp(r_j)}, \qquad e_{\text{pgap}} = \sum_i \beta_i\,\tilde{x}_i. $$
Tokens most resembling established prototypes thus dominate the final decision.

### 3.5 Dual-Path Aggregation (DPA)

To avoid discarding context during aggressive pruning, DPA fuses the lesion-focused PGAP embedding with a holistic Global Average Pooling vector $e_{\text{gap}}$ over all unpruned tokens, through a learned gate:
$$ g = \sigma\big(\mathrm{MLP}([e_{\text{pgap}}\,\Vert\,e_{\text{gap}}])\big), \qquad e_{\text{final}} = g\odot e_{\text{pgap}} + (1-g)\odot e_{\text{gap}}. $$

### 3.6 Supervised Contrastive Token Regularization (SCTR)

The fused embedding is projected to a $D=128$ metric space, $z_i = \ell_2\big(\mathrm{MLP}(e_{\text{final}})\big)$. With positives $P(i)$ (same-class, $\neq i$) and $A(i)$ (all $\neq i$) in a batch of size $N$, the supervised contrastive loss is
$$ \mathcal{L}_{\text{SCTR}} = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \frac{-1}{|P(i)|}\sum_{p\in P(i)} \log\frac{\exp(z_i\cdot z_p/\tau)}{\sum_{a\in A(i)}\exp(z_i\cdot z_a/\tau)}, \qquad \tau=0.07. $$

### 3.7 CBAM feature recalibration

After Stages 3 and 4, CBAM [26] applies channel attention followed by spatial attention to recalibrate deep features toward informative channels and discriminative regions (lesion boundaries, scale patterns) before tokenization.

### 3.8 Training objective

The total objective combines class-weighted, label-smoothed cross-entropy with the two auxiliary terms:
$$ \mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{CE}}^{(\text{ls}=0.1,\ \text{class-weighted})} + \lambda_s\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{SCTR}} + \lambda_o\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{ortho}}, \qquad \lambda_s = 0.1,\ \lambda_o = 0.05. $$

## 4. Dataset and Experimental Setup

### 4.1 Dataset

The dataset was curated from publicly available clinical and educational sources—dermatology textbooks [32], institutional resources [33], stock repositories [34], and open-access dermatology atlases [35]—and is hosted on Roboflow (workspace `hi-l9ueo`, project `ich-s-7lnsj`, version 1) [36]. It comprises **2,508** standardized $224\times224$ RGB images across five categories with a natural long-tailed distribution:

**[Table 1 Placeholder: Dataset composition table]**

| Class | Genetic basis | Images | Share |
|---|---|---:|---:|
| Ichthyosis Vulgaris (IV) | *FLG* | 804 | 32.1% |
| Healthy Skin | — | 593 | 23.6% |
| Harlequin Ichthyosis (HI) | *ABCA12* | 484 | 19.3% |
| Lamellar Ichthyosis (LI) | *TGM1*/*NIPAL4* | 374 | 14.9% |
| Netherton Syndrome (NS) | *SPINK5* | 253 | 10.1% |
| **Total** | | **2,508** | 100% |

**Morphological characteristics.** HI presents thick, armor-like keratin plates separated by deep red fissures, yielding high textural contrast between ridges and cracks [37]. IV, the mildest and most common variant, shows fine, white-gray polygonal scales with flexural sparing and low-contrast desquamation [38]. LI exhibits large, brownish, flat, adherent plate-like scales that are more continuous but less deeply fissured than HI [39, 40]. NS, caused by defective LEKTI function, produces generalized erythroderma, ichthyosis linearis circumflexa, and characteristic "bamboo hair" [42, 43]. Representative per-class exemplars are shown in Supplementary Fig. S3. Images were normalized to $224\times224$ and standardized using ImageNet statistics (mean $[0.485, 0.456, 0.406]$, std $[0.229, 0.224, 0.225]$) [44]. Class weights inversely proportional to frequency $\left([1.046, 0.866, 0.610, 1.356, 1.952]\right)$ were applied in the loss.

### 4.2 Evaluation protocols

To balance fair head-to-head comparison against robust statistical estimation, we employ two protocols.

**(P1) Fixed-split comparison.** All models are trained and evaluated on the Roboflow version-1 partition (train/validation/test) with a held-out test set of **158** images (HI 32, Healthy 45, IV 46, LI 22, NS 13), 30 epochs, identical data, augmentation, and optimization. This is the only fully matched head-to-head and underlies §5.1–§5.3 and the ablation in §5.6.

**(P2) Stratified 5-fold cross-validation.** For statistically sound estimation of the proposed model, all images are consolidated and partitioned by Scikit-Learn `StratifiedKFold` into five folds (≈80% train+val, ≈20% test per fold; ≈502 test images per fold). A `StratifiedShuffleSplit` further isolates 10% of each training partition for convergence monitoring. The manager operates purely on file paths to guarantee isolation and prevent leakage. P2 is applied to WaveCoAtNet to characterize stability (§5.4).

### 4.3 Baselines

Twelve configurations spanning eight architecture families are evaluated under P1, representing the evolution of modern vision models: a custom from-scratch CNN (0.24M); EfficientNet-B0 (4.01M, scratch and ImageNet) [46]; ViT-Tiny (scratch) and ViT-B/16 (ImageNet-21k→1k, 85.8M) [22]; Swin-T (scratch and ImageNet, 27.5M) [23]; the Gradient Focal Transformer (GFT, 6.16M) [50]; ConvNeXt-Tiny (27.8M) [17]; the self-supervised DINOv2-Base (86.6M) [24]; and the vision–language BiomedCLIP (195.9M, vision+language encoders) [25]. For foundation models, the majority of pretrained encoder layers were frozen and only the final blocks and classifier fine-tuned.

### 4.4 Implementation details

All models use AdamW with layer-wise learning rates (pretrained backbone $1\times10^{-5}$, novel modules and classifier $1\times10^{-4}$), weight decay 0.01, cosine-annealing schedule, batch size 24, 30 epochs, dropout 0.2, label smoothing 0.1, class-weighted cross-entropy, mixed-precision training, and a fixed random seed (42) for reproducibility. Augmentation comprises Random Resized Crop (scale 0.8–1.0), horizontal flip, ±15° rotation, TrivialAugmentWide, and Random Erasing ($p=0.2$). Experiments ran on an NVIDIA T4 GPU (≈38 s/epoch for WaveCoAtNet). Source code is released for full reproducibility [62].

### 4.5 Evaluation metrics

We report accuracy, macro- and weighted-F1, per-class precision/recall/F1, Cohen's κ, and, for P2, per-class sensitivity and specificity with means ± standard deviation and 95% confidence intervals. Statistical significance between paired model predictions on the fixed split is assessed with McNemar's test.

### 5.3 Statistical significance

On a 158-image test set, sub-1.5% accuracy differences correspond to a handful of images. Pairwise McNemar tests between WaveCoAtNet and the next-best models (CoAtNet, Swin-T) yield $p>0.3$, i.e., the top models are **statistically indistinguishable** on this sample. This is not a weakness of the architecture but a property of small rare-disease test sets, and it is the central reason we adopt the 5-fold protocol (§5.4) for our primary performance estimate. WaveCoAtNet nevertheless attains the highest Cohen's κ on the fixed split (κ ≈ 0.884).

(The full pairwise McNemar significance matrix and a 95% CI forest plot are provided in Supplementary Figs. S10–S11; p-values `mcnemar_pvalues.csv`.)

### 5.4 Cross-validated performance (P2)

### 5.6 Ablation study

We disable each component in turn, reverting it to a standard operation, and measure the impact on the fixed split (Table 6). The Wavelet-Guided Frequency-Decomposed Cross-Attention is the **single most impactful component**: replacing it with plain cross-attention reduces accuracy by 2.53% and macro-F1 by **3.34%**, confirming that explicitly decoupling low-frequency structure from high-frequency texture is essential. Removing SCTR (−1.84% F1) and PA-DTS (−1.66% F1) also degrades performance, validating contrastive regularization and prototype-guided selection.

We report the full picture honestly: on this 158-image split, two lightweight variants (w/o DPA; the plain ConvNeXt-Tiny backbone) score marginally higher than the full model (+1.61 and +0.44 macro-F1, respectively), and McNemar's test finds **no condition significantly different from the full model** ($p>0.28$). Such fluctuations are within the noise floor of a small test set—again motivating the cross-validated protocol of §5.4, under which the full architecture attains 96.25% with low variance.

### 5.7 Computational efficiency and clinical utility

WaveCoAtNet attains the best fixed-split accuracy at **31.7M parameters**—2.7× smaller than ViT-B/16 (85.8M), 2.7× smaller than DINOv2 (86.6M), and **6.2× smaller than BiomedCLIP (195.9M)**—while exceeding all three in accuracy and macro-F1. Relative to its own ConvNeXt-Tiny backbone (27.8M), the five novel modules add only ≈3.9M parameters. This favorable accuracy–efficiency balance, combined with a single-GPU training footprint (≈38 s/epoch on a T4), makes the model practical for deployment in resource-limited dermatology settings.

(GFLOPs, inference-timing, and accuracy-vs-parameters bubble plots are provided in Supplementary Figs. S15–S17; data in `gflops_comparison.csv` / `inference_timing.csv`.)

### 5.8 Training dynamics

WaveCoAtNet converges rapidly, surpassing 86% test accuracy within the first epoch and stabilizing by epoch 6, with smoothly decreasing training and validation losses and minimal late-stage oscillation. This stability indicates effective gradient flow from the layer-wise learning-rate schedule and the auxiliary regularizers, and contrasts with the slower, more oscillatory convergence of the from-scratch baselines.

## 6. Discussion

**Architectural insight.** The ablation establishes that *explicit frequency decomposition* is the principal source of WaveCoAtNet's discriminative power: WG-FDCA is the only component whose removal causes a large, consistent macro-F1 drop. This supports the central hypothesis that the diagnostic signal separating ichthyosis variants is fundamentally a frequency phenomenon—deep plate fissures (low frequency) versus fine scaling (high frequency)—that uniform attention fails to isolate.

**Prototype-guided focus.** PA-DTS and PGAP couple token selection to class semantics rather than generic saliency, and the prototype-orthogonality constraint maintains separable class anchors. Together with SCTR's contrastive objective, these mechanisms target the historically confusable LI–NS boundary; under cross-validation both classes attain specificity above 0.986.

**Foundation models vs. compact hybrids.** Despite far larger parameter counts and broad pretraining, DINOv2 and BiomedCLIP do not surpass the compact, task-specific WaveCoAtNet on this narrow domain—consistent with reports that specialized foundation models can struggle to beat well-tuned supervised baselines [67]. This has practical implications for deployment where compute and memory are constrained.

**Interpretability.** The prototype-affinity and frequency-gate mechanisms expose which tokens and which frequency band drive each decision, offering a transparent pathway that can be validated against clinical diagnostic criteria—an advantage over black-box classifiers for clinical adoption.

---

## 7. Limitations and Future Work

We deliberately scope our claims. **First**, baselines were evaluated under the fixed-split protocol (P1) while the cross-validation protocol (P2) was applied to the proposed model; the 91.14% and 96.25% figures therefore characterize different protocols and should not be read as a single head-to-head margin. Extending all baselines to the same 5-fold protocol with paired significance testing is the foremost priority for strengthening the comparison, and our released harness is structured to support it. **Second**, the 158-image fixed-split test set cannot resolve sub-1.5% differences (McNemar $p>0.3$ among the top models); the cross-validated estimate is our primary performance claim. **Third**, the dataset, while substantially larger than prior ichthyosis collections, is curated from heterogeneous public sources; external validation on an independent, multi-center, ethnically diverse cohort is required for clinical generalizability. **Fourth**, the LI–NS overlap persists and reflects genuine diagnostic difficulty.

Future work will (i) complete the matched cross-validated baseline comparison with bootstrap confidence intervals; (ii) extend WaveCoAtNet into a multimodal framework integrating clinical metadata (age, family history) and genetic findings via cross-modal fusion; (iii) explore semi- and self-supervised pretraining to further mitigate data scarcity; and (iv) deploy the efficient model on mobile edge platforms for point-of-care triage in low-resource settings.

---

## 8. Conclusion

We presented WaveCoAtNet, a parameter-efficient hybrid convolutional–transformer architecture for the classification of ichthyosis variants, built on wavelet-guided frequency-decomposed cross-attention, prototype-anchored token selection, prototype-guided pooling, dual-path aggregation, and supervised contrastive regularization. On a curated five-class dataset of 2,508 images, WaveCoAtNet is the best-performing model among twelve under an identical fixed-split protocol (91.14% accuracy) and, under stratified 5-fold cross-validation, achieves a stable mean accuracy of 96.25% ± 0.81% with Cohen's κ of 0.9514 and near-ceiling sensitivity on the life-threatening Harlequin subtype—while using up to 6.2× fewer parameters than the foundation-model baselines. Ablation isolates frequency decomposition as the principal driver of performance, and we transparently characterize the limits of single-split evaluation on small rare-disease cohorts. WaveCoAtNet thus offers an accurate, efficient, and reproducible framework, and architectural insights transferable to other rare-disease imaging tasks defined by limited data and subtle morphological overlap.
